Who should be screened for cancer

Cancer screening guidelines vary based on the type of cancer and your situation. People age 20 and older should ask a healthcare provider if they should have a cancer-related checkup. Healthcare providers use these checkups to determine if people have cancer risk factors. A risk factor is something that increases the chance you’ll have cancer. A family history of cancer is a risk factor.

That said, people generally don’t need regular cancer screening tests until they’re in their 40s. There are exceptions, including:

You’ve been diagnosed with a pre-cancerous condition.
You have a family history of cancer, particularly cancer diagnosed in younger family members.
Benefits of Immunotherapy
  • Screening can identify cancer at an early stage
  • Early intervention often leads to better treatment success rates and improved survival rates.
  • Some tests can detect precancerous conditions, allowing for measures to prevent cancer.
Downsides of Immunotherapy
  • Screening can sometimes indicate cancer when none is present
  • Certain tests, like biopsies, may have risks such as infection, discomfort, or complications.
  • Screening may cause anxiety or emotional distress for some individuals

Common cancer Screening tests

Physical examinationDuring your routine physical exam, a healthcare provider may check for any changes in your body, such as unusual lumps. They will also ask about your lifestyle habits and your family's medical history.

Laboratory testsThese tests may involve blood tests for cancer, tissue collection procedures, and urine tests. For instance, Pap smears are a type of screening test used to collect tissue samples for medical pathologists to analyze for signs of cancer.

Imaging tests These tests are used to capture detailed images of the inside of your body to help detect abnormalities. These tests are often employed in cancer screening and diagnosis. For example, a mammogram is a common imaging test specifically designed to screen for breast cancer.

Genetic tests for cancer If someone in your biological family has a type of hereditary cancer, your provider may recommend genetic tests to see if you have an increased risk of developing that cancer.

Breast cancer screening tests

Breast cancer screening, including mammograms and MRIs for high-risk individuals, detects cancer early to improve outcomes.

Prostate cancer screening tests

PSA measures prostate-specific antigen levels in the blood, and the DRE, a physical exam to detect abnormalities. Screening helps with early detection but may lead to false positives or overdiagnosis.

Cervical cancer screening tests

Cervical cancer screening detects early changes in cervical cells to prevent or treat cancer effectively. Tests include Pap smears to identify abnormal cells and HPV tests to detect the virus that can cause cervical cancer

Lung cancer screening tests

Lung cancer screening uses low-dose computed tomography (LDCT) to detect lung cancer at an early, more treatable stage. Regular screening can improve survival rates through early detection.

Colorectal cancer tests

Colorectal cancer tests, such as colonoscopy and stool-based tests, detect early signs of cancer or precancerous polyps, aiding prevention and early treatment.

Ovarian cancer screening tests

Screening test includes transvaginal ultrasound and blood tests like CA-125, aim to detect cancer in high-risk individuals, though routine screening is not widely recommended.

Harnessing Your Immune System to Fight Cancer

Immunotherapy is a groundbreaking cancer treatment that leverages the power of your immune system to detect and destroy cancer cells. Unlike normal cells, which naturally die off, cancer cells multiply uncontrollably, often mutating to evade immune detection.

Immunotherapy drugs are specially designed to alert the immune system to these rogue cells, enabling it to identify and eliminate them effectively. By enhancing the body’s natural defenses, this treatment offers a targeted approach to combat cancer while sparing healthy tissues.

Benefits of Immunotherapy
  • Targets cancer cells with high precision, minimizing damage to healthy tissue.
  • Can provide long-lasting effects, offering durable remission.
  • Works synergistically with other treatments like chemotherapy or radiation.
Downsides of Immunotherapy
  • May cause immune-related side effects, like inflammation or organ damage.
  • Not effective for all patients or types of cancer.
  • Expensive and can require long treatment durations.

Types of Immunotherapy

Immune Checkpoint Inhibitors - These drugs block immune checkpoint proteins (e.g., PD-1, PD-L1) that prevent the immune system from attacking cancer cells, allowing for stronger immune responses against tumors. They have shown promising results in cancers like melanoma, lung cancer, and more.

Monoclonal Antibodies - Laboratory-made antibodies designed to target specific proteins on cancer cells or pathogens, marking them for destruction by the immune system. Some monoclonal antibodies also deliver toxic agents directly to cancer cells to kill them.

T-Cell Therapy (e.g., CAR-T) - A treatment where a patient’s T cells are modified to express chimeric antigen receptors (CARs) to better recognize and attack cancer cells. It has shown significant success in treating blood cancers like leukemia and lymphoma.

Cancer Vaccines - These vaccines stimulate the immune system to recognize and target specific cancer-related antigens, prompting an immune response against the tumor. They can be used both as preventive vaccines (e.g., HPV vaccine) and therapeutic vaccines for existing cancers.

Immunotherapy Treatment Methods

Immunotherapy enhances the body's immune system to combat diseases, including cancer. It works by stimulating or restoring the immune system's natural ability to detect and destroy abnormal cells.

Intravenous (IV) Infusion:

Administered through a vein infusion (intravenously), using a needle inserted into a vein in the arm or chest.

Oral Administration

Some immune-modulating drugs are available in pill form, used for specific checkpoint inhibitors or targeted therapies..

Intratumoral Injection

Immunotherapy is injected directly into the tumor, used in some cancer vaccines and experimental treatments.

Intravesical Injection

Delivered directly into the bladder for localized treatment, commonly used in bladder cancer immunotherapy.

Topical Application

Certain immunotherapies, like creams or ointments, are applied directly to the skin, used for skin cancers or localized conditions.

Subcutaneous Injection:

Immunotherapy is injected under the skin, often used for certain monoclonal antibodies and cytokine therapies.
